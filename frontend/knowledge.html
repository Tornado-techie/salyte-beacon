Frequently Asked Questions

Quick answers to common water safety questions

The World Health Organization (WHO) recommends a pH range of 6.5 to 8.5 for drinking water. Water outside this range may corrode pipes, affect taste, or indicate contamination. Regular pH testing helps ensure water safety and system integrity.

For private wells, test annually for bacteria, nitrates, and total dissolved solids. Test every 3 years for other contaminants. Municipal water should be tested if you notice changes in taste, odor, or appearance. High-risk areas may require more frequent testing.

Common contaminants include bacteria (E. coli, coliform), chemicals (chlorine, fluoride), heavy metals (lead, mercury), nitrates from fertilizers, and physical particles causing turbidity. Each requires specific testing and treatment methods.

The most effective method depends on specific contaminants. Reverse osmosis removes most dissolved solids, UV sterilization kills bacteria/viruses, activated carbon removes chlorine and organics. Multi-stage systems combining methods often provide the best protection.

Communities can form water committees, apply for grants, implement rainwater harvesting, maintain existing infrastructure, and educate residents about water conservation and quality. Partnerships with NGOs and government programs can provide technical and financial support.

First, stop using the water for drinking and cooking. Switch to bottled water immediately. Contact local health authorities and a certified water treatment professional. Retest after treatment to confirm contamination is resolved before resuming use.
